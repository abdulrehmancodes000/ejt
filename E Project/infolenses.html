How Does Blue Light Affect Us?

Blue light can harm the eyes as it causes stress, fatigue, and soreness. These symptoms can lead to long-term damage to the eyes and may deteriorate the vision over time. Nowadays, most of us spend hours working on our smartphones, laptops, and tablets. Even though most devices come with blue light filters, they do not provide adequate protection from blue light. Therefore, most people opt for blue ray glasses to protect the eyes. These screen protection glasses reflect the harmful blue-violet light rays and prevent them from reaching the eyes.

( FAQs )

1. Is it harmful to wear blue light lenses? +

Since blue light emits from all sources, you can wear blue light protection glasses all day long. These glasses do not harm the eyes in the long term and are suitable for people of all ages. In addition, these anti-blue light eyeglasses prevent blurry vision, watering of eyes, and fatigue. Therefore, it is suitable to wear them all day or when you use your digital devices.

2. Should you wear blue light lenses to watch TV or laptop? +

LED screens emit blue light that can harm the eyes when it reaches the retina. This light can deteriorate the vision over time and cause other symptoms as well. Therefore, it is beneficial to wear glasses with a blue cut lens for optimum protection.

3. Can blue light lenses make you dizzy? +

Blue filter glasses do not have any adverse effects and they do not make one feel dizzy. Hence, you can wear them all day long without worrying about side effects.

4. When should I start to wear blue light lenses? +

Most people spend eight to nine hours of the day in front of a digital screen. You can wear blue cut glasses to prevent these symptoms and protect the eyes.

5. Can blue light lenses cause headaches? +

Prolonged exposure to blue light hinders vision and can lead to headaches. You can wear blue light blocking glasses to prevent these headaches.

6. Does blue light affect hormones? +

Blue light disrupts the release of the melatonin hormone in the body, which is responsible for sleep cycles.
